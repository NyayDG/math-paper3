Derivatives and Differentiation

Derivability and Derivative:
Derivability refers to the ability of a function to have a derivative.
A derivative, denoted by f'(x) or dy/dx, gives the rate of change of a function with respect to its independent variable.
It tells us how much the function changes for a small change in its input variable.

Derived Function:
The derived function is the derivative of the original function.
It is obtained by finding the derivative of a function with respect to its independent variable.
For example, if f(x) = x^2, then f'(x) = 2x.

Derivability Implying Continuity:
A function is said to be continuous at a point if the limit of the function at that point
exists and is equal to the value of the function at that point.
If a function is differentiable at a point, then it is also continuous at that point.

Geometrical Interpretation of a Derivative:
The derivative of a function at a point represents the slope of the tangent line to the graph of the function at that point.
The tangent line touches the graph of the function at only one point, unlike a secant line which passes through two points of
the graph. The derivative gives us the instantaneous rate of change of the function at a particular point.

Higher Order Derivatives:
The derivative of a derivative is called a second-order derivative.
The derivative of a second-order derivative is called a third-order derivative, and so on.
Higher order derivatives are useful in analyzing the behavior of a function, especially at critical points like maxima and minima.

Leibnitz Theorem:
Leibnitz's theorem provides a method to find the derivative of a product of two functions.
It states that the derivative of the product of two functions is equal to the first function
times the derivative of the second function plus the second function times the derivative of the first function.
It is a useful tool for solving problems involving products of functions.

Real-World Applications:
The concept of derivatives and differentiation is used extensively in physics, engineering,
and economics to model and analyze the behavior of systems.
For example, the rate of change of velocity with respect to time gives acceleration in physics.
The concept of derivatives is also used in finance to model and analyze financial derivatives such
as options and futures contracts.

Example:
Suppose you are driving a car, and your speedometer reads 50 km/h.
The speedometer gives you the average speed over a short interval of time.
However, to know your instantaneous speed, you need to find the derivative
of the distance traveled with respect to time.
If the distance traveled is given by the function f(t) = 5t^2, where t is time in seconds, then the
derivative of f(t) gives you the instantaneous speed of the car at any time t.

Expansion of Functions, Tangents, and Normals

Maclaurin's Theorem:
Maclaurin's theorem is a special case of Taylor's theorem, where the center of
the approximation is at x = 0.
It states that any infinitely differentiable function can be represented by a
power series expansion at x = 0.
The coefficients of the power series are given by the derivatives of the
function evaluated at x = 0.

Taylor's Theorem:
Taylor's theorem is a mathematical formula that allows us to approximate a
function with a polynomial.
It states that any infinitely differentiable function can be represented by
a power series expansion around a given center x = a.
The coefficients of the power series are given by the derivatives of the
function evaluated at x = a.

Equations of Tangent and Normal:
The tangent to a curve at a point is a straight line that touches the curve at
that point and has the same slope as the curve at that point.
The equation of the tangent at a point (a, f(a)) is given by y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a),
where f'(a) is the derivative of the function at point a.
The normal to a curve at a point is a straight line that is perpendicular
to the tangent at that point.
The equation of the normal at a point (a, f(a)) is given by y - f(a) = -1/f'(a)(x - a),
where f'(a) is the derivative of the function at point a.

Real-World Applications:
The concept of Taylor series expansion is widely used in physics, engineering,
and computer science to approximate complex functions with simpler ones.
For example, the Taylor series expansion of sine function is used to approximate
oscillatory behavior in electrical circuits and mechanical systems.
The concept of tangents and normals is used in computer graphics to simulate the
reflection of light rays off curved surfaces.

Example:
Suppose you have a function f(x) = x^2. To find the equation of the tangent and
normal to the curve at the point x = 1, we need to first find the derivative of the function,
which is f'(x) = 2x. Evaluating f'(1) gives us the slope of the tangent and normal at the point x = 1,
which is 2.

The equation of the tangent is y - 1 = 2(x - 1), or y = 2x - 1.
The equation of the normal is y - 1 = -1/2(x - 1), or y = -1/2x + 3/2.

Mean Value Theorems

Rolle's Theorem:
Rolle's theorem is a theorem in calculus that states that if a function f(x)
is continuous on the closed interval [a, b], differentiable on the open interval
(a, b), and f(a) = f(b), then there exists at least one point c in (a, b) such that f'(c) = 0.
In other words, if a function takes the same value at two points,
then there must be a point in between where the derivative is zero.

Lagrange's Mean Value Theorem:
Lagrange's mean value theorem is a theorem in calculus that states that if a
function f(x) is continuous on the closed interval [a, b], differentiable on
the open interval (a, b), then there exists at least one point c in (a, b)
such that f'(c) = [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a).
In other words, the theorem guarantees the existence of a point c in (a, b)
where the slope of the tangent to the curve equals the average rate of change
of the function over the interval [a, b].

Cauchy's Mean Value Theorem:
Cauchy's mean value theorem is a generalization of Lagrange's mean value theorem.
It states that if two functions f(x) and g(x) are continuous on the closed
interval [a, b], differentiable on the open interval (a, b), and g'(x) is
non-zero for all x in (a, b), then there exists at least one point c in (a, b)
such that [f(b) - f(a)]/[g(b) - g(a)] = f'(c)/g'(c).
In other words, the theorem guarantees the existence of a point c in (a, b)
where the ratio of the change in f to the change in g equals the ratio of the
derivative of f to the derivative of g.

Real-World Applications:
The mean value theorem is used in physics to describe the behavior of objects in
motion, such as the position, velocity, and acceleration of an object over time.
It is also used in economics to model changes in production and consumption over time.
